The /

Unlike with the other areas, my experiential learning at Virginia Tech was lacking; after placing so much focus on my academic success and social activity levels, I began to run out of steam and failed to take full advantage of the professional learning opportunities available to me within the Department of Psychology. For several years I maintained a student job with the Virginia Tech Transportation Institute as a Data Reductionist in which I observed, documented, and codified driver behaviors under naturalistic study settings for nation-wide transportation research projects. This was very rewarding work that gave me valuable knowledge about practical research methods, introduced me to a number of data analysis skills, and significantly helped further develop my computer skills.

However, I relied on this job during school to provide me with all of the opportunities and knowledge that I would need to succeed after graduation, and as a result I did not put reach out to professors in the psychology department, nor did I seek out internships with outside companies. This decision prevented me from getting up-close and personal with ongoing research projects and from developing firsthand knowledge of how upper-level employees operate in the professional setting. This time around will be better utilized with my renewed focus on career development and experience-building.
